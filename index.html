Route

Øygardstøl → three ridges w/ chains → Kjerag plateau → Nesatindane viewpoint → Kjeragbolten → 1,075 m high point → Varmekrokheia plateau → Langavatn (DNT cabin area)
The first half is the hard half: exposed granite slabs, steep up-and-down, chain sections, rock that's lethal when wet, and open windswept plateau. After Kjeragbolten it's still ~3 h to Langavatn. Rated expert-level with a full pack.

Camping at Langavatn

  • Camp on durable ground away from the cabin entrance, water points & the trail.
  • Right-to-roam: ≥150 m from cabins/houses, ≤2 nights same spot, pack out all rubbish + toilet paper.
  • Follow any on-site DNT signs — terrain/rules may restrict pitching.
  • Toilet: the Langavatn DNT cabin typically has an outhouse — use it if available (confirm on arrival). Toilets also at Øygardstøl car park & Lysebotn campsite. If none available, dig a cathole ≥60 m from water/trail/cabin with a trowel — and pack out used toilet paper in a sealed bag rather than burying it (barely decomposes in cold alpine soil).
  • Water: treat all lake/stream water — filter or purification tablets, not assumed safe as-is.
Langavatn Turisthytte is a DNT self-service cabin (unstaffed, locked with the DNT key). We're not carrying a DNT key, so treat the cabin as not accessible to us — the tent is the shelter, full stop. If the weather turns badly overnight, the fallback is: sit tight in a well-pitched tent, don't cross the plateau in poor visibility, and call 113 in a real emergency. (If someone happens to be staying in the cabin they might let you in, but don't plan around it.)

🗣️ Ask the locals for the go/no-go: Øygardstøl café/parking staff Friday morning are your decisive source — they see the actual rock/fog conditions and everyone coming off the mountain. Also ask the Sirdal fjellpark hosts Thursday night for the general read. Treat their word as overriding the forecast — if they say the slabs are soaked / it's blowing hard up top, that's a Plan A day (or wait a few hours). If parking staff advise against going, don't go.

Slippery rock is the real danger

Not just heavy rain — risk spikes when it rained overnight, rock is still wet, wind is up, or low cloud kills visibility. The chains get far more slippery wet. If parking staff / local authorities advise against going, follow them.

Bad-weather alternatives

Plan A — drop the mountain night: Fri = classic Kjerag round trip only (~11 km, 6–10 h), back to Øygardstøl, sleep at Lysebotn.
Plan B — skip Kjerag: strong wind / steady rain / thunder / car park or trail closed → don't enter the plateau. Rest in Lysebotn, head back to Stavanger early, pick a low-altitude walk.
Plan C — weather turns after Langavatn: shelter in a well-pitched tent (no DNT key, so the locked cabin isn't an option); don't cross the plateau in very poor visibility; delay departure until it clears; in emergency call 113.

Pack-carrying safety on the chains

  • Heavy items close to your back; nothing loose inside.
  • Stow trekking poles for chain sections.
  • Keep distance; don't share the same chain section at once; never pull each other by the pack.
🆘 Norway emergency: 113. Øygardstøl car park is ~7.5 km above Lysebotn, has toilets; no toilets or bins on the trail. Parking ≈300 NOK/vehicle (confirm nearer the date).

🎒 Packing & equipment

Target pack weight: ~10–12 kg dry, ~13–15 kg with water+food — ideally ≤20–22% of body weight. The chains aren't for an 18–20 kg pack; split shared kit (tent, stove, cooking) between you.
Pack size: 45 L + a small front pack (or a 38 L) is fine if your gear fits — no need for 50–65 L. But anything strapped outside (sleeping bag, mat) must go in a dry bag (Friday rain + wet chains), and be cinched tight so it can't swing or snag on the chain scrambles.

Essentials (★ = critical, ticks saved on this device)

Even late July, the ~1000 m plateau can be cold, windy, wet & foggy. Don't pack for Stavanger's temperature.

🍫 Food & water

Water

  • Start each day with 1.5–2 L — the first half (three ridges + exposed granite plateau) is dry, so carry enough to get across it.
  • Refill in the second half: a stream in the greener valley toward Langavatn, and Langavatn itself is a lake — plenty of water at camp. Filter it (BeFree AC).
  • Don't assume any source is safe untreated, especially near trails/cabins/animals.
  • Electrolyte powder.

Food — 2 hiking days + 1 emergency meal

  • Day 1 lunch + dinner; Day 2 breakfast + lunch.
  • 2–3 high-energy snacks per person per day.
  • 1 spare freeze-dried emergency meal.
  • Aim ~2,500–3,200 kcal/person/day.

Good options: freeze-dried meals, oats, nuts, energy bars, chocolate, dried sausage / hard cheese, crackers or flatbread, electrolyte drinks, instant coffee sachets.
